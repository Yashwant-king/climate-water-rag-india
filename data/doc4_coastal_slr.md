# Report: Sea-Level Rise and Coastal Water Security (Updated 2026)

## Overview
India's extensive coastline is facing an existential threat from climate change-induced Sea-Level Rise (SLR). The compounded effect of warming oceans (thermal expansion) and melting polar ice is critically threatening coastal megacities like Mumbai, Chennai, and Kolkata.

## Mumbai: Infrastructure and Inundation
Recent 2026 projections by the National Institute of Oceanography (NIO) highlight severe risks:
- **Sea-Level Projection**: By 2050, the mean sea level around Mumbai is projected to rise by up to 0.45 meters relative to 2005 levels.
- **Inundation Risk**: Low-lying areas such as South Mumbai, Juhu, and the newly built coastal road corridors face chronic high-tide flooding. This is lethal when SLR coincides with extreme monsoon cloudbursts, overwhelming the city's century-old drainage systems.
- **Economic Loss**: Over $15 billion in real estate and infrastructure value is at direct risk of permanent or frequent inundation by mid-century.

## Chennai: The "Day Zero" Threat and Salinity
Chennai's water crisis is a complex mix of urban sprawl, poor watershed management, and coastal geography.
- **Over-extraction**: Depletion of groundwater has lowered the hydraulic head, pulling the Bay of Bengal's saltwater 10-15 km inland into the Minjur aquifer. 
- **Desalination Dependance**: To counter the freshwater deficit, Chennai has radically scaled up reverse-osmosis desalination (e.g., the 400 MLD Perur plant capacity). However, these plants consume massive amounts of energy and discharge hyper-saline brine back into the ocean, damaging local marine biodiversity.
- **Cyclonic Contamination**: Intense storm surges during cyclones temporarily wipe out freshwater lenses in the coastal sands.

## East Coast vs. West Coast
- The **East Coast** (Bay of Bengal) is experiencing a relatively higher rate of relative sea-level rise compared to the West Coast (Arabian Sea), making cities like Kolkata and Chennai highly susceptible to both slow-onset inundation and sudden cyclonic surges.

## Key Statistics (2026 Baseline):
- Projected SLR by 2050 (Mumbai): up to 0.45 meters.
- Current intrusion distance (Chennai/Minjur): ~10-15 km inland.
- Economic risk (Coastal India): Scaled to $45 billion by 2050 without adaptation.
